# Binaural Studio Tone of Voice and AI Writing Guide

This file is the primary writing reference for Binaural Studio. Use it whenever creating, researching, outlining, drafting, editing, or reviewing content for the website, audio library, experiments, email, social media, or future products.

The goal is not to make every piece sound identical. The goal is to keep the brand recognizable, credible, and human across different formats and AI tools.

---

## 1. Brand foundation

### What Binaural Studio is

Binaural Studio is being built as a combination of:

- An educational publisher
- A free functional audio library
- A public experimentation platform

The educational layer builds trust. The audio library gives people something useful to try. The experimentation layer creates differentiation through documented methods, firsthand experience, and eventually original data.

Binaural Studio is not primarily an app at launch. An app may eventually support the experience, but it should not define the brand or its voice.

### Central promise

Binaural Studio helps people understand functional audio, try it responsibly, and determine whether it is useful for them.

A fuller expression of the promise is:

> Binaural Studio helps people explore how sound may support focus, rest, and mental state through clear education, transparent audio experiments, and practical listening tools.

### Editorial posture

Binaural Studio investigates. It does not advocate for every claim made about binaural beats or functional audio.

The brand is interested without being convinced, open-minded without being credulous, and willing to say when evidence is limited or conflicting.

The working posture is:

> Curious, open-minded, and unwilling to exaggerate.

### Core philosophy

> Try it. Observe it. Understand the limits. Keep what helps.

This philosophy should guide both content and product decisions.

## 2. Audience

### Primary audience at launch

Write primarily for people who are new to binaural beats or functional audio and are interested in:

- Focus and productivity
- Relaxation and rest
- Better digital routines
- Understanding what they hear
- Testing whether audio helps them personally

The initial audience includes curious knowledge workers, creators, students, and self-improvement-minded people who already use headphones while working or relaxing.

Focus and productivity should lead much of the initial positioning. These topics fit the founder's experience, create fewer medical implications than sleep or anxiety, and provide strong opportunities for practical tools and experiments.

### Secondary audiences

Secondary readers may include:

- Meditation users
- People exploring audio for sleep
- Neurodivergent listeners
- Audio and neuroscience enthusiasts
- Skeptical readers evaluating popular claims
- People interested in spiritual interpretations of sound

Do not imply that Binaural Studio was created to diagnose, treat, or support a medical condition.

### Reader mindset

Most readers will arrive curious, hopeful, and confused. Some will be skeptical. Others may already believe strong claims.

Common questions include:

- Do binaural beats actually work?
- Which frequency should I use?
- Do I need headphones?
- Can this help me focus?
- Is this safe?
- How is this different from brown noise?
- What does a particular frequency supposedly do?

Respect both skepticism and hope. Do not ridicule people for what they believe. Do not validate unsupported conclusions simply because they are popular.

### Assumed scientific literacy

Assume the reader is interested but nontechnical.

Explain concepts clearly enough for a beginner while framing them accurately enough that a knowledgeable expert would not object.

Technical terms may be used when helpful, including:

- Frequency
- Carrier tone
- Beat frequency
- Brainwave band
- Placebo
- Sample size
- Statistical significance
- Entrainment
- Psychoacoustics

Define unfamiliar terms in plain English the first time they appear.

## 3. Core voice

### Voice in one paragraph

Binaural Studio sounds like a curious, well-informed guide exploring functional audio alongside the reader. The writing is calm, direct, and transparent about what is known, what remains uncertain, and what comes from personal experience. It takes research seriously without becoming academic, respects spiritual and personal interpretations without presenting them as scientific fact, and helps readers run thoughtful experiments instead of promising dramatic results.

### Primary voice attributes

#### Curious, not convinced

Explore questions rather than forcing conclusions.

Use:
- "Researchers have tested..."
- "One possible explanation is..."
- "The results are worth examining..."
- "We do not yet have enough evidence to conclude..."

Avoid:
- "Science has proven..."
- "This frequency definitely..."
- "The truth is..."
- "This is the only method that works..."

#### Calm, not sleepy

The brand should feel composed and unhurried, but the writing still needs momentum.

Use clear sentences, practical examples, and intentional pacing. Do not make every paragraph meditative or atmospheric.

#### Scientific, not clinical

Use evidence and precise language without sounding like a journal article or medical institution.

Translate research rather than copying its language. Explain study limitations in ways ordinary readers can understand.

#### Open-minded, not credulous

Personal experiences and spiritual interpretations may be meaningful. They are not automatically scientific evidence.

Acknowledge them respectfully, then identify what category of claim is being discussed.

#### Practical, not prescriptive

Help readers test audio and observe their own response.

Prefer:
- "Try this during one defined work session."
- "Keep the volume comfortable."
- "Compare the experience with and without the track."

Avoid:
- "Listen for exactly 30 minutes to activate..."
- "You need this frequency to..."
- "Everyone should..."

#### Warm, not overly personal

The writing should feel human. It may include founder observations, sensory descriptions, and limited humor.

Do not turn every article into a personal essay or make the founder's experience the primary proof.

#### Modern, not trend-driven

The brand should understand current wellness, productivity, neuroscience, and audio conversations without chasing every viral claim.

#### Confident, not absolute

Make clear recommendations where the evidence and context support them. State uncertainty directly when they do not.

Measured language should feel intentional, not timid.

## 4. What makes Binaural Studio distinct

### Translate the space instead of selling certainty

Much of the category falls into one of two styles:

- Mystical language that assigns dramatic effects to frequencies
- Clinical summaries that explain research but give the reader little practical help

Binaural Studio should sit between them.

The brand explains:

- What the claim means
- Where the claim came from
- What research has measured
- What the research has not established
- What a listener can reasonably try
- How to observe the result without overinterpreting it

### Treat audio as something made

The word "Studio" should communicate craft, experimentation, sound design, and transparent construction.

Content should explain details such as:

- Carrier tones
- Beat frequency
- Stereo separation
- Headphone requirements
- Masking sounds
- Session length
- Volume
- Why two tracks labeled with the same beat frequency may feel different

This gives the brand firsthand authority beyond summarizing other articles.

### Treat listening as an experiment

Do not frame every track as a guaranteed solution.

A useful track description explains:

- What the track was designed for
- How it was constructed
- How to use it
- What the listener might observe
- What should not be assumed
- How the experience could be compared

Example:

> This session uses a 14 Hz binaural beat beneath a low-distraction ambient soundscape. Try it during a defined work period and note whether it helps you settle into the task. The frequency does not guarantee a particular mental state, and individual responses may differ.

### Separate different kinds of evidence

When relevant, distinguish among:

1. Established findings
2. Preliminary or mixed research
3. Plausible theory
4. Traditional or spiritual belief
5. Personal observation
6. Marketing claim
7. Unsupported assertion

Do not blend these categories together.

Useful editorial labels may include:

- What we know
- What studies suggest
- What listeners report
- What remains uncertain
- How to try it
- Founder observation
- Expert perspective
- Track design notes

## 5. Claims and evidence standards

### General standard

Binaural Studio should be more conservative with claims than most competitors.

Do not make a statement stronger than the evidence supporting it.

### Avoid claims such as

- Proven to rewire your brain
- Guaranteed to improve focus
- Heals anxiety
- Cures insomnia
- Raises your vibration
- Activates a specific brain state
- Unlocks hidden mental capacity
- Scientifically proven, unless the exact claim is genuinely established
- Forces your brain into a frequency
- Works for everyone

### Prefer language such as

- May help
- Has been studied for
- Some participants reported
- Researchers observed
- Evidence is mixed
- Early findings suggest
- This has not been established conclusively
- Individual responses vary
- The study found an association
- The result has not been consistently replicated
- This is a proposed mechanism, not a settled explanation

### Do not over-caveat

Accuracy does not require a disclaimer after every sentence.

State the limitation once, clearly, near the relevant claim. Then continue with useful explanation.

The goal is measured clarity, not defensive writing.

### Use topic-driven safety and efficacy coverage

Safety and efficacy are important editorial responsibilities, but they are not mandatory sections in every article.

Use three levels of coverage:

1. **Dedicated coverage:** Maintain a clear site disclaimer and substantial evergreen pages about evidence, safe listening, side effects, and when professional guidance may be appropriate. These pages are the primary destinations for complete baseline guidance.
2. **Reusable contextual note:** Place a short expandable listening note near audio players, interactive demonstrations, and other places where a reader can immediately play sound. Link it to the dedicated safety page. This interface note should not become repeated article copy.
3. **Article-specific coverage:** Discuss safety or efficacy in the article body only when it is central to the search intent, necessary to qualify a claim, or needed because the page gives instructions that could create a meaningful risk.

Do not add a generic safety section, hearing reminder, or “results may vary” paragraph merely to satisfy a template. Deep explainers, technical articles, history pages, experiment reports, and frequency pages may stay focused on their actual subject when the relevant limitations are already handled at the claim, interface, or site level.

Examples:

- A safety article should cover hearing protection and adverse effects in depth.
- An article asking whether binaural beats work should discuss evidence and uncertainty in depth.
- A frequency page should qualify claims made about that frequency, but it does not need to repeat the full safe-listening guide.
- A technical article about carrier tones does not need a separate efficacy section unless it makes an outcome claim.
- Any page with playable audio should expose the reusable listening note near the player.

Site-wide disclaimers do not excuse unsupported claims. Qualify a claim where it appears, but do not repeat unrelated caveats throughout the page.

### Research hierarchy

For scientific and health-related claims, prefer:

1. Systematic reviews and meta-analyses
2. Peer-reviewed controlled studies
3. Academic or government explanations
4. Qualified expert commentary
5. Reputable secondary reporting
6. Manufacturer or app claims, clearly identified as such
7. Anecdotes, clearly labeled

Do not treat all sources as equally credible.

### Reading studies accurately

When reviewing research:

- Identify what outcome was actually measured
- Note the sample size
- Check whether there was a control group
- Distinguish self-reported outcomes from physiological measurements
- Avoid generalizing from a narrow population
- Note whether the result was statistically significant
- Look for replication or conflicting findings
- Separate the authors' result from broader interpretations

Do not convert "participants reported lower anxiety after one session" into "binaural beats treat anxiety."

### Medical boundaries

Binaural Studio is educational and experimental, not medical advice.

Do not:

- Diagnose conditions
- Recommend replacing treatment
- Suggest audio is a cure
- Imply a user should ignore symptoms
- Write as though Binaural Studio is a clinical authority

When a topic has meaningful safety or treatment implications, recommend consultation with an appropriate qualified professional.

## 6. Spiritual and cultural topics

Binaural Studio may cover manifestation, chakras, altered states, solfeggio frequencies, meditation traditions, and related topics because readers search for and use them.

Handle these topics respectfully while separating belief from evidence.

A useful pattern is:

> This interpretation is meaningful within certain spiritual traditions, but it has not been established through scientific research.

Do not:

- Mock spiritual users
- Present spiritual beliefs as neuroscience
- Use scientific terminology to make a belief sound proven
- Dismiss subjective meaning simply because it is not measurable

Where appropriate, explain that a ritual, expectation, or listening context may still influence a person's experience without proving the underlying claim.

## 7. Founder voice and point of view

### Use "I" for

- Personal listening experiments
- Founder observations
- Firsthand process notes
- Explaining why the project exists
- Describing what was tested or changed

Example:

> I used this track during five focused work sessions and recorded how distracted I felt before and afterward.

### Use "we" for

- Binaural Studio's editorial position
- Published testing methods
- Recommendations developed by the publication
- The audio design or review process
- Findings from a documented Binaural Studio experiment

Example:

> We found that the existing studies were too inconsistent to support a broad claim about improved concentration.

Do not use "we" to imply a research team, laboratory, medical organization, or large editorial staff that does not exist.

### Preserve the founder's real perspective

When Drew provides rough notes, interview answers, or observations:

- Preserve distinctive phrasing where it sounds natural
- Clean up grammar and repetition
- Do not flatten the perspective into generic wellness language
- Keep uncertainty and skepticism when they are part of the original thought
- Verify factual claims separately from personal observations

The founder is an investigator and practitioner, not a neuroscientist. Credibility comes from transparent methods, careful sourcing, and honest interpretation.

## 8. Tone by content type

The voice stays consistent, but the tone should adapt to the reader's situation.

### Educational explainers

Examples:
- What are binaural beats?
- How do binaural beats work?
- Binaural beats vs. brown noise

Tone:
- Clear
- Direct
- Patient
- Evidence-aware
- Beginner-friendly

Prioritize definitions, distinctions, and practical understanding.

### Research summaries

Examples:
- What research says about binaural beats and focus
- A review of recent binaural beat studies

Tone:
- Precise
- Transparent
- Slightly more formal
- Careful about causation
- Explicit about limitations

Avoid turning an abstract into a conclusion stronger than the authors made.

### Audio and track pages

Tone:
- Practical
- Calm
- Slightly sensory
- Specific about construction and use
- Clear about intended purpose

Include listening instructions and design notes. Do not promise an outcome.

### Experiments

Tone:
- Transparent
- Methodical
- Curious
- Firsthand
- Comfortable reporting a null or ambiguous result

Include:
- Question
- Method
- Conditions
- What was measured
- Results
- Limitations
- What may be tested next

Do not present a personal test as clinical research.

### Founder notes

Tone:
- Personal
- Reflective
- Honest
- Less formal
- Still disciplined about factual claims

### Safety and health-related content

Tone:
- Calm
- Clear
- Direct
- Non-alarmist
- Appropriately cautious

### Audio instructions

Tone:
- Reassuring
- Simple
- Unhurried
- Specific

Example:

> Set the volume low enough that the tones never feel sharp or tiring. Give yourself a few minutes before deciding whether the track is helping.

### Social content

Tone:
- Concise
- Curious
- Slightly more conversational
- Specific enough to teach something
- Never sensationalized for clicks

Avoid turning caveated findings into definitive hooks.

## 9. Writing style

### General style

Use:

- Active voice
- Plain English
- Contractions where natural
- Varied sentence and paragraph length
- Specific examples
- Clear distinctions
- Concrete verbs
- Occasional light skepticism
- Sensory language where it supports an audio experience
- A real point of view

Avoid:

- Academic imitation
- Wellness clichés
- Hype
- Throat-clearing introductions
- Empty transitions
- Overly polished AI prose
- Repetitive conclusions
- Formulaic balance
- Artificially poetic descriptions
- Constant rhetorical questions
- A disclaimer-heavy tone

### Sentence and paragraph rhythm

Most paragraphs should contain two to four sentences, but rhythm should vary naturally.

Avoid:

- Every paragraph having the same length
- A long series of one-sentence paragraphs
- Multiple consecutive sentences beginning with the same word
- Ending every paragraph by restating its first sentence
- Always grouping ideas into three

A one-sentence paragraph may be used occasionally for genuine emphasis, but not as a repeated AI-style device.

### Headlines

Use sentence case.

Headlines should tell readers what a section helps them understand. They may be direct or lightly curious, but not clickbait.

Prefer:
- Why headphones are required for binaural beats
- What a 10 Hz beat does and does not mean
- The evidence is more mixed than the marketing
- How to test a focus track for yourself

Avoid:
- Unlock the secret power of 10 Hz
- The ultimate guide to brainwave transformation
- This frequency will change your life

### Analogies

Use analogies when they make acoustics or research easier to understand.

Keep them accurate and brief. Do not let the analogy become the explanation.

### Humor

Use limited, gentle humor. It may challenge exaggerated claims without challenging the reader.

Suitable:

> No, a single tone is unlikely to unlock 100 percent of your brain. It may still make an afternoon work session feel easier.

Avoid sarcasm, ridicule, or jokes about mental health, disability, sleep problems, spiritual practices, or a reader's intelligence.

## 10. How not to sound AI-generated

AI may assist with research, organization, drafting, and editing. The final writing should sound like a thoughtful person made specific decisions.

### Do not use generic AI openings

Avoid:

- In today's fast-paced world
- In an increasingly digital age
- Have you ever wondered
- In this article, we will explore
- Let's dive in
- Binaural beats have gained significant popularity
- For centuries, humans have been fascinated by sound
- Whether you're looking to focus, relax, or sleep

Open with a useful question, a reader problem, a surprising distinction, a concrete observation, or a claim worth examining.

### Avoid empty interpretive sentences

Do not write:

- This is important.
- That matters.
- This highlights the importance of...
- This underscores...
- This reflects...
- This represents...
- This demonstrates the power of...

State the implication directly.

Instead of:

> This highlights the importance of wearing headphones.

Write:

> Without separate tones reaching each ear, the binaural effect is not created.

### Avoid canned contrast patterns

Do not repeatedly use:

- It's not X. It's Y.
- This isn't just X. It's Y.
- Not only X, but also Y.
- The question isn't X. The question is Y.

Use a direct statement instead.

### Avoid uniform structure

AI writing often creates:

- Three examples in every list
- Identical paragraph lengths
- Predictable H2 sections
- A summary sentence after every idea
- Balanced treatment of every possible viewpoint
- A conclusion that repeats the introduction
- "Firstly, secondly, finally" sequencing

Vary the structure based on the subject. Take a position when the evidence supports one.

### Avoid polished vagueness

Words that sound professional but often hide weak thinking should be replaced with specific language.

Avoid or use rarely:

- Delve
- Harness
- Illuminate
- Pivotal
- Realm
- Underscore
- Tapestry
- Leverage
- Robust
- Revolutionize
- Synergy
- Cutting-edge
- Game-changing
- World-class
- Ultimate
- Transformative
- Powerful, unless describing measurable power or volume
- Optimize, when "improve" or a specific action is clearer

### Avoid stock phrases

Do not use:

- At its core
- That being said
- To put it simply
- It's important to note that
- Navigate the complexities of
- Shed light on
- In conclusion
- To summarize
- The bottom line is, unless it genuinely fits the voice
- Moreover
- Furthermore
- Additionally
- Consequently

### Do not fabricate human texture

Do not invent:

- Personal anecdotes
- Listener stories
- Study outcomes
- Test results
- Expert quotes
- Placeholder people
- Specific experiences Drew has not reported

Human writing comes from real observations, not invented detail.

### Keep useful imperfections

Do not polish away every trace of personality. Natural writing may include:

- A clear preference
- A brief aside
- A sentence with a little skepticism
- Uneven paragraph lengths
- An admission that a result was disappointing or unclear
- A direct statement that a popular claim is unsupported

### Edit for decisions, not synonyms

A human edit should ask:

- What is this paragraph trying to do?
- Is the claim specific?
- Does the evidence support it?
- Would a reader know what to do next?
- Is this section necessary?
- Does the wording sound like Binaural Studio?
- Is there a more honest or useful way to frame this?

Replacing words with synonyms is not enough.

## 11. Blog writing workflow

Use this workflow when creating or substantially revising a Binaural Studio article.

### Phase 1: Clarify the article

Identify:

- Topic or working title
- Intended reader
- Search intent, if SEO is a goal
- Reader's practical question
- Business or editorial purpose
- Article type
- What claim or assumption the article needs to examine

Ask questions only when missing information would materially change the direction. Otherwise, state the assumptions and move forward.

### Phase 2: Research the current landscape

Research is required when the topic depends on:

- Current scientific literature
- Recent studies
- Health or safety guidance
- Search results
- Competitor claims
- Product or platform changes
- Current cultural trends
- A specific paper, expert, or publication

For SEO-focused content, identify:

- Search intent
- What currently ranks
- Common angles
- Repeated weaknesses
- Unsupported claims appearing in the results
- Questions competitors leave unanswered
- Opportunities for original diagrams, tests, or tools
- Primary and secondary topics

Do not fabricate keyword volume, difficulty, rankings, study details, or statistics.

### Phase 3: Gather firsthand perspective

When the article benefits from original experience, ask Drew focused questions such as:

- What made you interested in this question?
- What have you noticed while using or creating this type of audio?
- Which popular explanation seems misleading?
- How would you test this claim personally?
- What result would make you change your mind?
- What limitation should readers understand?
- What would you tell someone trying this for the first time?

After the interview, summarize:

- Core thesis
- Original angle
- Founder perspective
- Examples or tests to include
- Phrases worth preserving
- Claims requiring verification
- Gaps that need expert input

Do not force founder commentary into topics where it adds no value.

### Phase 4: Build the article strategy

Before drafting, establish:

- Target reader
- Search and reader intent
- Core promise
- Recommended conclusion or position
- Evidence needed
- Original contribution
- Practical takeaway
- Visual opportunity
- Internal links
- Appropriate CTA

Possible article types include:

- Beginner explainer
- Research review
- Myth or claim examination
- Practical listening guide
- Experiment report
- Track design breakdown
- Comparison
- Founder perspective
- Expert interview
- Resource page

### Phase 5: Draft

Create a complete draft that:

- Answers the primary question early
- Distinguishes evidence from interpretation
- Includes a real point of view
- Gives the reader something practical to do
- Includes firsthand experience only when real
- Uses source-backed claims
- Avoids repetitive AI patterns
- Matches the tone required by the content type

### Phase 6: Humanize and verify

Before finalizing:

- Remove filler openings and transitions
- Vary paragraph length
- Break predictable groups of three
- Replace vague claims with specific ones
- Remove repeated summaries
- Verify every scientific or medical statement
- Check that source language has not been overstated
- Preserve useful founder phrasing
- Read the draft aloud for rhythm
- Confirm that the article does not sound more certain than the evidence

### Phase 7: Package for publishing

Only when requested, include:

- Page title
- Slug
- Meta title
- Meta description
- Post summary
- Author
- Published and updated dates
- Category
- Estimated read time
- Main image direction
- Image alt text
- Social share image direction
- Article body
- Internal links
- External sources
- Reviewer or expert attribution
- AI transparency note

## 12. Recommended blog structure

Structure should follow the question, not a rigid template. For a standard educational article, use this as a starting point.

### 1. Direct opening

Open in one or two sentences with:

- The practical question
- A common misunderstanding
- A useful distinction
- A specific observation
- The claim being evaluated

Do not begin with broad historical context unless history is central to the article.

### 2. Early answer

Give the reader a concise answer or framing near the beginning.

Do not force them through 800 words before answering the title.

### 3. Necessary explanation

Define the concept in plain language. Explain enough science or acoustics to support the rest of the article.

### 4. Evidence and limitations

Explain what has been studied, what was measured, and where findings agree or conflict.

### 5. Practical application

Show the reader how to use, test, compare, or think about the audio.

Include at least one of:

- Listening protocol
- Checklist
- Comparison table
- Step-by-step experiment
- Example scenario
- Diagram
- Track construction notes

### 6. Binaural Studio perspective

Add a clear editorial conclusion, founder observation, experiment, or design perspective when it provides original value.

### 7. Closing

Land the main idea or suggest a reasonable next test.

Do not recap every section. Do not use "In conclusion."

### 8. CTA

Use a helpful, context-specific CTA.

Examples:

- Try the related track
- Compare two audio types
- Join an experiment
- Record a listening result
- Read the underlying research review
- Explore the audio library
- Subscribe for future tests

Do not force a commercial CTA where a useful next step is more appropriate.

## 15. Examples of on-brand and off-brand writing

### Explaining a concept

Off-brand:

> Binaural beats harness the incredible power of sound to synchronize your brainwaves and unlock elevated states of focus.

On-brand:

> A binaural beat is created when each ear receives a slightly different tone. The listener perceives the difference as a rhythmic pulse, although researchers are still studying how consistently that experience affects attention or mood.

### Describing research

Off-brand:

> Science proves that theta binaural beats reduce anxiety and promote deep relaxation.

On-brand:

> Some studies have reported lower self-rated anxiety after binaural beat sessions. The evidence is not consistent enough to treat that result as guaranteed or as a substitute for clinical care.

### Describing a track

Off-brand:

> Activate laser focus with this scientifically engineered 14 Hz frequency.

On-brand:

> This track uses a 14 Hz binaural beat beneath a restrained ambient layer. It was designed for focused work, but the frequency alone does not guarantee concentration. Try it during a defined task and compare the experience with your usual work setup.

### Addressing a spiritual claim

Off-brand:

> The 528 Hz frequency repairs DNA and raises your vibration.

On-brand:

> The idea that 528 Hz has special healing properties is common in spiritual and wellness content. It has not been established through reliable scientific evidence, although some listeners may still find the music or ritual personally meaningful.

### Founder observation

Off-brand:

> Our groundbreaking tests reveal that binaural beats are the future of productivity.

On-brand:

> In my early work sessions, the track seemed most useful as a cue to begin focusing. I cannot tell from that experience whether the binaural beat itself caused the change, so future tests should compare it with a similar track that does not contain the beat.

## 17. Source and citation expectations

Every published article containing factual, scientific, safety, historical, or market claims should cite reliable sources.

Use sources to support the writing, not decorate it.

Citations should be close to the claim they support. Do not place a long source list at the end while leaving the reader unable to tell which source supports which statement.

When sources disagree:

- Say that they disagree
- Explain the likely reason when known
- Avoid selecting only the result that supports the preferred conclusion
- Distinguish quality differences among studies
- State the editorial interpretation separately

Do not cite an article that cites a study when the original study is accessible and understandable enough to use directly.

## 18. AI transparency

Binaural Studio may use AI as part of research organization, interviewing, drafting, editing, formatting, or production.

AI should not be presented as the author of firsthand experiences, original experiments, or expert conclusions.

For publish-ready articles, include a brief transparency note when appropriate:

```text
---
AI Transparency

Tools used: [list tools]
How AI was used: [brief factual description]
Research review and final edit: Drew Slade, Binaural Studio
Expert review: [name and credentials, if applicable]
---
```

Do not imply an expert reviewed content unless that review occurred.
